Job Coach — Visual Direction

Phase 4, Step 1: Three Visual Directions

Grounded in the three references you sent. Before any component gets built, pick the direction that's right — everything downstream (buttons, forms, cards, the coach interface) gets designed once, in that direction, not three times.

React Bits reactbits.dev

Dark ground, animated shader/gradient backgrounds, vivid purple — motion is the product here. Read as: creative-developer, maximalist, playground energy.

Symbol Studio symbolstudio.pl

Neutral paper/near-black grounds, one vivid orange-red accent, a custom geometric display face at large scale. Read as: premium branding studio, editorial, confident.

Vivid Motion vividmotion.co

Near-black ground, vivid red-orange accent, editorial serif display + grotesque sans body + monospace for data labels. Read as: cinematic, premium, restrained-until-it-isn't.

Two of three independently converge on a vivid orange-red accent against a neutral or near-black ground, paired with editorial serif display type. That's the DNA all three directions below share. What differs is how far each one leans into dark and motion — the two things that make sense on a portfolio site but need real judgment on a tool someone uses daily to manage something as stressful as a job search.

Aa Editorial serif for headlines and moments that need weight — profile milestones, the coach's key recommendations. Clean grotesque sans carries every UI surface and body of text. C — Calibrated Warmth

light-first · one signature motion moment

The middle path. Warmer paper than A, a softer accent than B, and motion spent in exactly one place — the coach's "thinking" state — instead of spread thin or absent. Built for daily use by someone who's stressed, not for a portfolio scroll.

Recommendation Direction C — Calibrated Warmth. This product gets used repeatedly, often under real stress, by people reading dense information (CVs, job requirements, salary fields) in two languages. Direction B is the most striking of the three and worth strong consideration if you want the product to feel more "creative-tech, tech-savvy Ahmed" from the first screenshot — but it asks the most of accessibility and RTL testing, and dark-mode-first tools can read less "official" for a career decision. Direction A is the safest and cheapest to execute well, but risks blending into generic SaaS. C keeps the premium DNA your references share, spends its one motion moment where it earns trust (the coach thinking), and is built to still feel calm on a user's twentieth session, not just their first.

Design-system approach, once you pick

StepWhat happens
1. Direction lockedFull token set finalized: exact color scale (not just 4 swatches), type scale, spacing scale, radius/elevation rules — for both light and dark, whichever direction is chosen
2. Core componentsButtons, forms, cards, job cards, the citation chip (Confirmed/Inferred), navigation, modals — built once against the 10 screen specs from Phase 3
3. Coach-specific componentsChat bubble, thinking-state animation, citation inline pattern — these are the components that make the product feel "AI-integrated," not "AI as a bolted-on chat window"
4. StatesEmpty, loading, error, permission states styled per the patterns defined in Phase 3
5. RTL passEvery component checked mirrored, not just the type
Pick a direction — A, B, C, or a specific mix (e.g. "C's palette with B's dark mode as an equal option") — and the full design system gets built against that choice, not all three.
